## 1. Regression in Feature Space and Kernel Formulation

### 1.1 Regularized least squares in feature space

We observe i.i.d. pairs

$$
\{(x_n,t_n)\}_{n=1}^N, \qquad x_n \in \mathbb{R}^P, \qquad t_n \in \mathbb{R},
$$

and we model the targets as

$$
t_n = \langle w,\phi(x_n)\rangle_{\mathcal H} + \eta_n,
\qquad
\eta_n \sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma_\eta^2).
$$

The unknown variable is the weight vector

$$
w \in \mathcal H.
$$

The regularized least-squares problem is

$$
\widehat w
=
\arg\min_{w \in \mathcal H}
\left[
\frac{1}{2}\sum_{n=1}^N
\bigl(t_n - \langle w,\phi(x_n)\rangle_{\mathcal H}\bigr)^2
\frac{\lambda}{2}\lVert w\rVert_{\mathcal H}^2
\right],
\qquad \lambda \ge 0.
$$

Equivalent compact form:

$$
\widehat w
=
\arg\min_{w \in \mathcal H}
\left[
\frac{1}{2}\lVert t - \Phi w\rVert_2^2
\frac{\lambda}{2}\lVert w\rVert_{\mathcal H}^2
\right],
$$

where the feature operator $\Phi$ is defined by

$$
(\Phi w)_n = \langle w,\phi(x_n)\rangle_{\mathcal H}.
$$

Role of the regularization parameter $\lambda$:

- it penalizes large norms of $w$ in feature space,
- it makes the problem better conditioned when $\Phi^\top \Phi$ is ill-conditioned or singular,
- it controls the bias-variance tradeoff,
- in kernel language it prevents the estimator from interpolating noisy targets too aggressively.

### 1.2 Closed-form solution in matrix notation

Assume first that the feature space is represented by $M$ coordinates. Then

$$
\Phi =
\begin{bmatrix}
\phi(x_1)^\top\\
\vdots\\
\phi(x_N)^\top
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb R^{N \times M},
\qquad
t =
\begin{bmatrix}
t_1\\
\vdots\\
t_N
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb R^N,
\qquad
w \in \mathbb R^M.
$$

The objective becomes

$$
J(w)
=
\frac{1}{2}(t-\Phi w)^\top (t-\Phi w)
+
\frac{\lambda}{2} w^\top w.
$$

Expand the first term:

$$
J(w)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left(
t^\top t - 2 t^\top \Phi w + w^\top \Phi^\top \Phi w
\right)
+
\frac{\lambda}{2} w^\top w.
$$

Differentiate with respect to $w$:

$$
\nabla_w J(w)
=
-\Phi^\top t + \Phi^\top \Phi w + \lambda w.
$$

Set the gradient to zero:

$$
-\Phi^\top t + \Phi^\top \Phi w + \lambda w = 0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
(\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda I_M)w = \Phi^\top t.
$$

If $\lambda > 0$, the matrix $\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda I_M$ is positive definite on the span of the data, so the estimator is

$$
\widehat w
=
(\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda I_M)^{-1}\Phi^\top t.
$$

In operator form, valid even when $\mathcal H$ is infinite-dimensional,

$$
\widehat w
=
(\Phi^\ast \Phi + \lambda I_{\mathcal H})^{-1}\Phi^\ast t.
$$

This is the regularized least-squares estimator in feature space.

### 1.3 Polynomial map, design matrix, and Python implementation

The polynomial feature map is

$$
\phi(x) =
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\
x\\
x^2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

For samples $\{(x_n,t_n)\}_{n=1}^N$, the design matrix is

$$
\Phi =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & x_1 & x_1^2\\
1 & x_2 & x_2^2\\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\
1 & x_N & x_N^2
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb R^{N \times 3}.
$$

The exam asks for an implementation. The repository now contains it in [solve_exam_01.py](/home/mitin/code/ml-theory/exams/01/solutions/code/solve_exam_01.py).

The core computation is:

```python
def build_polynomial_design_matrix(x_samples: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    x_vector = np.asarray(x_samples, dtype=np.float64).reshape(-1)
    return np.column_stack((np.ones_like(x_vector), x_vector, x_vector**2))


def solve_regularized_least_squares(
    design_matrix: np.ndarray,
    targets: np.ndarray,
    lambda_reg: float,
) -> np.ndarray:
    gram = design_matrix.T @ design_matrix
    rhs = design_matrix.T @ targets
    system = gram + lambda_reg * np.eye(design_matrix.shape[1], dtype=np.float64)
    return np.linalg.solve(system, rhs)
```

### 1.4 Kernel trick from the SVD of the design matrix

Let the design matrix admit the thin singular value decomposition

$$
\Phi = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^\top,
$$

where

- $U_r \in \mathbb R^{N \times r}$ has orthonormal columns,
- $V_r \in \mathbb R^{M \times r}$ has orthonormal columns,
- $\Sigma_r \in \mathbb R^{r \times r}$ is diagonal with positive singular values,
- $r = \operatorname{rank}(\Phi)$.

Then

$$
\Phi^\top \Phi = V_r \Sigma_r^2 V_r^\top,
\qquad
\Phi \Phi^\top = U_r \Sigma_r^2 U_r^\top.
$$

The second matrix is the Gram matrix

$$
K = \Phi \Phi^\top,
\qquad
K_{ij} = \langle \phi(x_i), \phi(x_j)\rangle_{\mathcal H} = k(x_i,x_j).
$$

Starting from the primal estimator,

$$
\widehat w = (\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda I)^{-1}\Phi^\top t,
$$

insert the SVD:

$$
\widehat w
=
\left(V_r \Sigma_r^2 V_r^\top + \lambda I\right)^{-1}
V_r \Sigma_r U_r^\top t.
$$

Restricted to the data span, this simplifies to

$$
\widehat w
=
V_r (\Sigma_r^2 + \lambda I_r)^{-1}\Sigma_r U_r^\top t.
$$

Now define dual coefficients

$$
\alpha = (K + \lambda I_N)^{-1} t.
$$

Using the SVD,

$$
\alpha
=
U_r (\Sigma_r^2 + \lambda I_r)^{-1} U_r^\top t
+
\text{components in the null space}.
$$

Multiplying by $\Phi^\top$ gives

$$
\Phi^\top \alpha
=
V_r \Sigma_r U_r^\top
U_r (\Sigma_r^2 + \lambda I_r)^{-1} U_r^\top t
=
V_r (\Sigma_r^2 + \lambda I_r)^{-1}\Sigma_r U_r^\top t
=
\widehat w.
$$

Hence

$$
\widehat w = \Phi^\top \alpha,
\qquad
\alpha = (K+\lambda I_N)^{-1}t.
$$

So the predictor for any new input $x$ is

$$
\widehat f(x)
=
\langle \widehat w,\phi(x)\rangle_{\mathcal H}
=
\left\langle \sum_{n=1}^N \alpha_n \phi(x_n), \phi(x)\right\rangle_{\mathcal H}
=
\sum_{n=1}^N \alpha_n k(x_n,x).
$$

In vector form,

$$
\widehat f(x) = k_x^\top (K+\lambda I_N)^{-1} t,
\qquad
k_x =
\begin{bmatrix}
k(x_1,x)\\
\vdots\\
k(x_N,x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This is the kernel trick: we never need to build $\phi(x)$ explicitly. Everything is expressed through inner products encoded by the Gram matrix $K$.

## 2. RKHS, Mean Embeddings, and Discrepancy Between Distributions

### 2.1 Empirical mean embedding and kernel evaluations

Let $k : \mathcal X \times \mathcal X \to \mathbb R$ be a positive-definite kernel with RKHS $\mathcal H_k$, and let $\phi(x)=k(x,\cdot)$ be the canonical feature map.

Given a sample $\{x_n\}_{n=1}^N$, the empirical mean embedding is

$$
\widehat \mu_X
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \phi(x_n)
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N k(x_n,\cdot)
\in \mathcal H_k.
$$

For any query point $z \in \mathcal X$, the reproducing property gives

$$
\langle \widehat \mu_X, k(z,\cdot)\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \langle k(x_n,\cdot), k(z,\cdot)\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N k(x_n,z).
$$

So the mean embedding can be evaluated using only kernel values.

#### Case i: linear kernel

With the linear kernel

$$
k(x_i,x_j) = x_i^\top x_j,
\qquad
x_i,x_j \in \mathbb R^P,
$$

the RKHS is just $\mathbb R^P$ with the standard inner product, and

$$
\phi(x)=x.
$$

Therefore the empirical mean embedding is

$$
\widehat \mu_X
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N x_n
=
\bar x
\in \mathbb R^P.
$$

Its evaluation at $z \in \mathbb R^P$ is

$$
\langle \widehat \mu_X, z\rangle
=
\bar x^\top z
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N x_n^\top z
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N k(x_n,z).
$$

Dimensions:

- $x_n,z \in \mathbb R^P$,
- $\widehat \mu_X \in \mathbb R^P$,
- the evaluation $\bar x^\top z$ is a scalar.

#### Case ii: Gaussian RBF kernel

For the Gaussian kernel

$$
k(x_i,x_j) = \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i - x_j\rVert_2^2),
\qquad
\gamma > 0,
$$

the RKHS is generally infinite-dimensional. The embedding is still

$$
\widehat \mu_X = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N k(x_n,\cdot),
$$

and the evaluation at a query $z$ is

$$
\langle \widehat \mu_X, k(z,\cdot)\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_n-z\rVert_2^2).
$$

Dimensions:

- $x_n,z \in \mathbb R^P$,
- $\widehat \mu_X \in \mathcal H_k$,
- the evaluation is again a scalar,
- in practice we manipulate the vector of kernel evaluations
  $k_z = [k(x_1,z),\ldots,k(x_N,z)]^\top \in \mathbb R^N$.

Interpretation:

- the linear kernel mean embedding is exactly the ordinary sample mean in the original coordinate system,
- the RBF kernel mean embedding captures nonlinear similarity structure and depends on local neighborhoods rather than only first-order Euclidean coordinates.

Numerical check from the script:

- for the sample $\{(0,1),(1,0),(2,1)\}$ and query $z=(1,2)$, the linear embedding equals the sample mean $(1,\tfrac{2}{3})$,
- the linear evaluation is $2.3333$ both from $\bar x^\top z$ and from the direct kernel average,
- with $\gamma=0.5$, the RBF evaluation at the same query is $0.2904$.

### 2.2 Raw, centered, and standardized moments

The difference is the same in the original space and in feature space, but the object whose moments are taken changes.

#### In the original space

Let $X \in \mathbb R^P$ be a random vector with mean $\mu = \mathbb E[X]$ and marginal standard deviations collected in a diagonal matrix $S$.

The $m$th raw moment is

$$
\mathbb E[X^{\otimes m}],
$$

which measures the moment relative to the origin.

The $m$th centered moment is

$$
\mathbb E[(X-\mu)^{\otimes m}],
$$

which measures variability around the mean instead of around the origin.

The $m$th standardized moment is

$$
\mathbb E\left[\left(S^{-1}(X-\mu)\right)^{\otimes m}\right],
$$

which removes both location and scale. Standardized moments isolate shape information such as skewness and kurtosis.

#### In the kernel-induced space

Now replace $X$ by the feature-space random element $\phi(X) \in \mathcal H_k$, and define

$$
\mu_\phi = \mathbb E[\phi(X)].
$$

Then:

- the raw moment is taken relative to the origin of $\mathcal H_k$,
- the centered moment is taken relative to $\mu_\phi$,
- the standardized moment additionally normalizes by feature-space variance.

One clean way to express this in RKHS is along any direction $u \in \mathcal H_k$:

$$
\text{raw: } \mathbb E[\langle \phi(X),u\rangle^m],
$$

$$
\text{centered: } \mathbb E[\langle \phi(X)-\mu_\phi,u\rangle^m],
$$

$$
\text{standardized: }
\mathbb E\left[
\left(
\frac{\langle \phi(X)-\mu_\phi,u\rangle}
{\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\langle \phi(X),u\rangle)}}
\right)^m
\right].
$$

So:

- raw moments depend on the choice of origin,
- centered moments remove mean effects,
- standardized moments remove both mean and scale effects,
- in RKHS this distinction is especially important because nonlinear feature maps can create large norm differences even when the original coordinates have already been centered.

### 2.3 RKHS discrepancy between two distributions and MNIST implementation

Let $X \sim P$ and $Y \sim Q$, with embeddings

$$
\mu_P = \mathbb E[k(X,\cdot)],
\qquad
\mu_Q = \mathbb E[k(Y,\cdot)].
$$

The squared RKHS discrepancy is

$$
\lVert \mu_P - \mu_Q\rVert_{\mathcal H_k}^2.
$$

Expand the norm:

$$
\lVert \mu_P - \mu_Q\rVert_{\mathcal H_k}^2
=
\langle \mu_P,\mu_P\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
+
\langle \mu_Q,\mu_Q\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
- 2\langle \mu_P,\mu_Q\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}.
$$

Using the reproducing property,

$$
\langle \mu_P,\mu_P\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\mathbb E[k(X,X')],
$$

$$
\langle \mu_Q,\mu_Q\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\mathbb E[k(Y,Y')],
$$

$$
\langle \mu_P,\mu_Q\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}
=
\mathbb E[k(X,Y)].
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{MMD}^2(P,Q)
=
\mathbb E[k(X,X')]
+
\mathbb E[k(Y,Y')]
- 2\mathbb E[k(X,Y)].
$$

#### Empirical matrix form

Let

$$
X_m = \{x_1,\ldots,x_m\}, \qquad Y_n = \{y_1,\ldots,y_n\}.
$$

Define

$$
K_{XX} \in \mathbb R^{m \times m},
\qquad
(K_{XX})_{ij} = k(x_i,x_j),
$$

$$
K_{YY} \in \mathbb R^{n \times n},
\qquad
(K_{YY})_{ij} = k(y_i,y_j),
$$

$$
K_{XY} \in \mathbb R^{m \times n},
\qquad
(K_{XY})_{ij} = k(x_i,y_j).
$$

Then the biased empirical estimator is

$$
\widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}_{\text{b}}^2
=
\frac{1}{m^2}\mathbf 1_m^\top K_{XX}\mathbf 1_m
+
\frac{1}{n^2}\mathbf 1_n^\top K_{YY}\mathbf 1_n
- \frac{2}{mn}\mathbf 1_m^\top K_{XY}\mathbf 1_n.
$$

An unbiased version removes the diagonal terms in $K_{XX}$ and $K_{YY}$, but the biased estimator is simpler and always nonnegative for a positive-definite kernel, so it is convenient for reporting.

## 3. Matrix Form of Kernels and Induced Geometry

### 3.1 Squared Euclidean distances in matrix form

Take two rows $x_i,x_j \in \mathbb R^P$. Then

$$
\lVert x_i - x_j\rVert_2^2
=
(x_i-x_j)^\top (x_i-x_j).
$$

Expand:

$$
\lVert x_i - x_j\rVert_2^2
=
x_i^\top x_i
+
x_j^\top x_j
- 2x_i^\top x_j.
$$

Now let

$$
X =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1^\top\\
\vdots\\
x_N^\top
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb R^{N \times P},
\qquad
G = XX^\top \in \mathbb R^{N \times N},
$$

and define the vector of squared norms

$$
s =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lVert x_1\rVert_2^2\\
\vdots\\
\lVert x_N\rVert_2^2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\operatorname{diag}(G)
\in \mathbb R^N.
$$

Let $\mathbf 1 \in \mathbb R^N$ be the all-ones vector. Then the full matrix of squared distances is

$$
D =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lVert x_1-x_1\rVert_2^2 & \cdots & \lVert x_1-x_N\rVert_2^2\\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
\lVert x_N-x_1\rVert_2^2 & \cdots & \lVert x_N-x_N\rVert_2^2
\end{bmatrix}
=
s\mathbf 1^\top + \mathbf 1 s^\top - 2XX^\top.
$$

Indeed, the $(i,j)$ entry of the right-hand side is

$$
s_i + s_j - 2x_i^\top x_j
=
\lVert x_i\rVert_2^2 + \lVert x_j\rVert_2^2 - 2x_i^\top x_j
=
\lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2.
$$

So the matrix formula is proved.

### 3.2 Gaussian kernel matrix: diagonal, symmetry, and positive semidefiniteness

For the Gaussian/RBF kernel,

$$
K_{ij} = \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2),
\qquad \gamma > 0.
$$

#### Diagonal entries

If $i=j$, then $\lVert x_i-x_i\rVert_2^2 = 0$, hence

$$
K_{ii} = \exp(0) = 1.
$$

Therefore all diagonal entries are the same constant:

$$
K_{11} = K_{22} = \cdots = K_{NN} = 1.
$$

Geometric meaning:

$$
K_{ii} = \langle \phi(x_i),\phi(x_i)\rangle_{\mathcal H}
=
\lVert \phi(x_i)\rVert_{\mathcal H}^2.
$$

So every transformed sample has the same RKHS norm:

$$
\lVert \phi(x_i)\rVert_{\mathcal H} = 1.
$$

This means all feature vectors lie on the unit sphere of the induced feature space.

#### Symmetry

Because Euclidean distance is symmetric,

$$
\lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2 = \lVert x_j-x_i\rVert_2^2,
$$

so

$$
K_{ij} = K_{ji}.
$$

Hence

$$
K = K^\top.
$$

#### Positive semidefiniteness

The Gaussian kernel is a positive-definite kernel. Therefore for any coefficients $c_1,\ldots,c_N \in \mathbb R$,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N c_i c_j k(x_i,x_j) \ge 0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
c^\top K c \ge 0
\qquad
\text{for all } c \in \mathbb R^N.
$$

So $K$ is positive semidefinite.

Because $k$ is positive definite, there exists a feature map $\phi$ such that

$$
K_{ij} = k(x_i,x_j) = \langle \phi(x_i),\phi(x_j)\rangle_{\mathcal H}.
$$

Therefore $K$ is precisely a Gram matrix in the induced feature space.

Numerical sanity check from the script:

- the maximum error between the direct distance computation and the matrix formula was about $5.68 \times 10^{-14}$,
- the maximum deviation of the RBF diagonal from `1` was about $5.55 \times 10^{-16}$,
- the symmetry error was exactly `0` up to machine precision,
- on the tested subset the minimum eigenvalue was approximately `0.0777`, which is consistent with positive semidefiniteness.

## Closing Summary

The exam connects four equivalent viewpoints:

- primal linear regression in feature space,
- dual/kernel regression through Gram matrices,
- RKHS mean embeddings and MMD as distribution-level objects,
- Gaussian kernels as distance-based Gram matrices with a clear induced geometry.

The key conceptual link is that once inner products in feature space can be written as kernel evaluations, the entire theory can be developed without explicitly constructing the feature map.
